PA6 vs PA66: How to Choose the Right Nylon for Injection-Molded Parts

The useful answer is not that one nylon is universally better. The right choice depends on service temperature, moisture exposure, stiffness, impact behavior, molding conditions, compliance needs, and the cost of keeping production stable.

Material SelectionBuyer GuideUpdated June 14, 2026

PA6 and PA66 are two of the most widely used engineering polyamides. Both can provide a strong combination of mechanical performance, wear resistance, chemical resistance, and efficient injection molding. Both are also available in unfilled, impact-modified, heat-stabilized, flame-retardant, mineral-filled, and glass-fiber-reinforced grades. That overlap is why buyers often receive several technically plausible quotations for the same part.

The decision becomes easier when the question changes from “Which nylon is better?” to “Which material system gives this part the most reliable performance and production window?” A resin family name alone cannot answer that. The final behavior depends on grade formulation, reinforcement, moisture condition, wall thickness, part geometry, molding history, and the test method used.

Quick answer for sourcing teams

PA66 is commonly shortlisted when higher thermal margin, stiffness, wear performance, or mechanical retention at elevated temperature matters. PA6 is often attractive when toughness, flow, processing flexibility, and commercial efficiency carry more weight. The decision must still be confirmed with grade-specific data and molded-part testing.

What is the practical difference between PA6 and PA66?

PA6 is produced from caprolactam, while PA66 is produced from hexamethylenediamine and adipic acid. Their molecular structures produce different melting, crystallization, moisture, and mechanical behavior. In purchasing language, PA66 often offers a higher melting range and a useful thermal-performance advantage. PA6 generally melts and processes at lower temperatures and can offer good toughness and flow.

Those statements describe broad families, not guaranteed values. A heat-stabilized PA6 grade can outperform a basic PA66 grade in a particular aging test. A toughened PA66 may behave very differently from an unmodified PA66. Glass content, flame-retardant chemistry, lubricant package, color, and recycled-content strategy can all shift the result. Buyers should compare candidate grades at the same conditioning state and using the same standard.

How should temperature requirements influence the choice?

Temperature is one of the clearest reasons to investigate PA66, but it is frequently specified too loosely. “Heat resistant” may mean a short peak during assembly, continuous operation near a motor, an under-hood aging requirement, a soldering process, or retention of clamp load after thousands of hours. These are not interchangeable.

A robust request for quotation should state the continuous service temperature, peak temperature, exposure duration, mechanical load, surrounding medium, and acceptable property loss. Heat deflection temperature can help compare materials under a defined laboratory load, but it does not by itself establish service life. For long-term applications, request heat-aging curves or retained-property data for the exact grade. If the part touches hot water, coolant, oil, or cleaning chemicals, ask for aging data in that medium rather than relying on dry-air data.

BASF describes unreinforced PA66 in its Ultramid A family as rigid, abrasion-resistant, heat-resistant, and suitable for mechanically and thermally stressed electrical, mechanical, and automotive parts. The same portfolio also shows why family-level descriptions are only a starting point: specialized reinforced and stabilized grades extend performance in very different directions.

Moisture, conditioning, and dimensional stability

Polyamides absorb moisture from the environment. Moisture can increase ductility and reduce stiffness; it can also change dimensions and electrical behavior. This is not automatically a defect. It is a material characteristic that must be included in drawing tolerances, test preparation, packaging, and end-use validation.

Problems arise when teams compare an oven-dry sample with a conditioned production part, or when one laboratory conditions specimens and another does not. A drawing may be accepted immediately after molding but drift after storage in a humid warehouse. A connector housing may meet insertion-force targets dry and respond differently after environmental exposure. A supplier change may appear to cause variation when the real difference is sample history.

For dimensional parts, define when measurements are taken and how specimens are conditioned. Record resin-lot identification, drying time, dew point where available, melt temperature, mold temperature, and the time between molding and inspection. For shipment inspection, moisture-sensitive resin should arrive in intact moisture-barrier packaging with clear lot labels and storage instructions. Opened bags should be controlled according to the supplier's technical guidance.

Strength, stiffness, impact, wear, and reinforcement

PA66 is often chosen for gears, brackets, housings, cable ties, clips, bearings, electrical components, and automotive parts because it can combine strength, rigidity, fatigue resistance, and wear performance. PA6 can serve many of the same applications. The meaningful comparison is therefore not neat PA6 against neat PA66 in isolation, but the actual candidate grades in the expected moisture and temperature condition.

Glass fiber raises stiffness, strength, creep resistance, and dimensional stability, but introduces anisotropy. Fibers align with melt flow, so shrinkage and strength become direction-dependent. More glass is not always better: a higher reinforcement level can reduce toughness, increase tool wear, expose fibers at the surface, complicate welding, and magnify warpage if the gate and geometry create unbalanced orientation.

For wear parts, do not select from a generic coefficient of friction. Counterface material, pressure, speed, temperature, lubrication, surface finish, and debris affect the result. A small application-representative wear test is often more useful than comparing isolated catalog values.

Processing and factory validation

Drying is a process variable, not a housekeeping detail

Excess moisture during melting can contribute to hydrolytic degradation, lower viscosity, flash, surface defects, and reduced mechanical performance. The correct drying temperature and time depend on the exact resin, packaging history, and dryer performance. A responsible factory trial records the material supplier's drying recommendation and verifies that the equipment can maintain the required dry-air condition. Simply heating resin in an uncontrolled oven is not equivalent to a validated drying process.

Compare candidates using the same mold and inspection plan

When possible, mold PA6 and PA66 candidates in the same tool under optimized but documented conditions. Compare fill pressure, cycle time, appearance, part weight, critical dimensions, flatness, weld lines, ejection behavior, and scrap. Then test functional performance after the agreed conditioning and aging sequence. One material may have a higher pellet price but deliver a lower reject rate or shorter cycle; another may meet every requirement with less processing energy.

Control changes before shipment

Commercial samples are not enough for ongoing supply. Agree on grade name, color code, packaging, lot traceability, certificate requirements, and change-notification expectations. Shipment inspection can include packaging integrity, label and batch verification, color comparison, pellet appearance, moisture or viscosity checks where appropriate, and retention samples. The inspection plan should match the risk of the part rather than create testing that has no decision value.

Application-based selection examples

Electrical connector or terminal housing

Start with operating voltage, temperature, wall thickness, flame classification, electrical tracking requirements, color, and terminal insertion process. PA66 may be a strong candidate, particularly in reinforced or flame-retardant form, but the exact certification and thickness are grade-specific. Confirm the relevant listing and molded-part performance.

Mechanical bracket or structural housing

Define sustained load, impact events, creep limit, flatness, fastener torque, and temperature. A glass-filled PA66 may provide valuable stiffness, while a PA6 system may offer sufficient performance with easier filling or better impact behavior. Gate position and fiber orientation can decide whether either material succeeds.

Gear, bushing, or sliding component

Document speed, load, counterface, lubrication, temperature, noise, and expected life. Material family comparisons are less reliable than application testing. Consider wear-modified grades and verify dimensional change after conditioning.

Consumer or appliance component

Surface quality, color consistency, odor, assembly, drop performance, and compliance may outweigh maximum stiffness. A mineral-filled or specially modified grade may be more suitable than a standard glass-filled compound.

Why total cost is more useful than resin price

The lowest price per kilogram can become expensive if the material needs a wider process adjustment, creates warpage, requires sorting, or causes field failures. Total molded-part cost includes resin consumption, density, cycle time, drying energy, scrap, machine and mold wear, inspection, secondary operations, logistics, and quality risk.

For an export program, also consider minimum order quantity, lead time, batch consistency, documentation, palletization, moisture protection, and the supplier's ability to communicate changes. A small price difference may be justified by a stable formulation and clear shipment documentation. Conversely, an over-specified material can lock unnecessary cost into every production cycle.

How to compare PA6 and PA66 supplier quotations

A technically useful quotation should identify more than polymer family and price. Ask each supplier to state the exact grade, manufacturer or formulation owner, reinforcement and modification type, color, packaging, minimum order, lead time, origin, recommended processing conditions, and the documents included with shipment. If a proposed material is described as “equivalent,” request a side-by-side comparison against the incumbent grade and make the supplier identify known differences rather than only matching a few headline values.

Normalize commercial comparisons. One quote may be ex-works and another delivered; one may include moisture-barrier bags, pallets, and certificates while another does not. Density can change the number of parts produced per kilogram. A grade that fills at lower pressure or allows a shorter cycle may reduce conversion cost. Build a comparison sheet with resin cost per molded part, trial cost, expected scrap, inspection, logistics, payment terms, and the cost of maintaining a second approved source.

Sample provenance should also be clear. Confirm that trial material represents the commercial formulation and expected production route. Record the sample lot and retain enough resin and molded parts for reference. After approval, connect the purchase order to the approved grade, color, packaging, and change-notification agreement. This prevents a successful laboratory sample from becoming an undefined commodity purchase six months later.

Buyer checklist before approving PA6 or PA66

  1. Describe the part, end-use industry, and failure mode that must be prevented.
  2. State continuous and peak temperature with duration, load, and contact media.
  3. Provide wall thickness, critical dimensions, tolerances, gate concept, and assembly method.
  4. List required standards and identify whether they apply to resin, specimen, or final part.
  5. Define dry and conditioned mechanical or dimensional requirements.
  6. Confirm color, surface, recycled-content, regrind, and appearance restrictions.
  7. Run a documented trial and inspect samples after the agreed conditioning period.
  8. Approve packaging, lot traceability, shipment inspection, and change control.

Frequently asked questions

Is PA66 stronger than PA6?

PA66 often shows higher stiffness and thermal margin in comparable unmodified forms, but “stronger” depends on grade, reinforcement, moisture, temperature, and test direction. Compare exact grades under the condition that represents the part.

Which material absorbs more moisture?

Both are hygroscopic and both require moisture-aware design and testing. Published values vary by grade and conditioning method. Use grade-specific data and define the measurement condition instead of relying on a single family-level number.

Can PA6 replace PA66?

Sometimes. A replacement is credible only after checking temperature, mechanical retention, dimensions, electrical or flame requirements, processing, and functional tests. A drop-in substitution should not be assumed.

Is PA66 always better for high-temperature parts?

No. PA66 is often a useful starting point, but stabilizer package, reinforcement, exposure medium, stress, and aging duration determine suitability. Higher-temperature polyamides or other polymers may be needed for severe conditions.

Should we choose 30% glass-filled PA66?

PA66 GF30 is common because it offers a practical stiffness-strength balance, but it is not a universal default. Thin walls, impact, surface quality, weld lines, warpage, and tool design may point to another reinforcement level or material system.

What information should be sent to a nylon supplier?

Send application, drawing or part photo, service environment, required standards, current grade, known problems, color, quantity, destination, and target timeline. Better input reduces sampling cycles and prevents price-only comparisons.

Conclusion: choose the validated material system

PA6 and PA66 are platforms, not single specifications. PA66 often deserves priority when thermal and mechanical margin are central; PA6 can be compelling where toughness, flow, processing, and cost balance dominate. The dependable answer comes from a written requirement, grade-specific evidence, controlled molding, conditioned-part testing, and disciplined supply approval.
