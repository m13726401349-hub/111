Glass-Filled PA66 Injection Molding: Defect Guide for Buyers and Molders

Warpage, exposed fiber, dimensional drift, short shots, and brittle parts rarely have one cause. Reliable troubleshooting separates material condition, part design, tooling, process settings, inspection timing, and shipment control.

Injection MoldingProblem-Solving GuideUpdated June 14, 2026

Glass-filled PA66 is used when an injection-molded part needs more stiffness, strength, creep resistance, heat performance, or dimensional stability than an unreinforced polyamide can provide. Common applications include automotive brackets, electrical housings, fan components, structural covers, appliance parts, gears, supports, and metal-replacement designs.

The same glass fibers that create performance also make molding behavior directional. Fibers align with flow, shrinkage differs along and across that direction, and the surface can reveal fiber-rich regions. PA66 also absorbs moisture, so resin drying and post-molding conditioning affect processing and inspection. When a part fails, changing material immediately can hide the real cause or move the defect somewhere else.

Quick troubleshooting rule

Freeze the evidence before changing settings: identify resin lot, moisture and drying history, machine and cavity, process record, part weight, defect location, measurement time, and environmental condition. Change one controlled factor at a time and compare against an approved reference.

What glass reinforcement changes in PA66

Glass fibers carry load and restrict polymer movement. As reinforcement rises, stiffness and strength generally increase, creep decreases, and thermal expansion can fall in the fiber direction. However, toughness, flow, surface quality, weld-line strength, and isotropy may become more difficult to manage. Fiber content alone does not define a grade. Fiber length distribution, coupling, base polymer viscosity, heat stabilization, impact modification, lubricant, pigment, and flame-retardant package all influence performance.

BASF describes glass-fiber-reinforced polyamides as offering high mechanical strength, rigidity, thermal stability, dimensional stability, and creep strength. Its public portfolio also identifies mineral- and glass-bead-filled systems as options where low warpage or surface appearance is important. This illustrates an important selection principle: if flatness is the dominant requirement, simply increasing long-glass or short-glass content may not be the best answer.

Why does PA66 GF30 warp?

PA66 GF30 is a common shorthand for PA66 reinforced with approximately 30% glass fiber by weight, but grades with the same nominal percentage can mold differently. Warpage develops when shrinkage is not balanced through the part. Fiber alignment reduces shrinkage along the flow direction more than across it. If the geometry or gate creates different orientation on opposite sides, the part bends or twists as it cools.

Uneven wall thickness adds another source of imbalance. Thick bosses cool and shrink differently from thin walls. Ribs may pull a broad surface. A gate at one end can create strongly oriented flow, while multiple gates can create weld lines and competing orientation. Mold-temperature differences between core and cavity, blocked cooling channels, inconsistent packing, and early ejection can all amplify the effect.

A disciplined warpage investigation

  1. Measure parts by cavity and record the time after molding.
  2. Check whether deformation follows the melt-flow direction.
  3. Compare actual part weight and packing consistency.
  4. Verify core and cavity surface temperature, not only controller settings.
  5. Review gate size, location, rib layout, wall transitions, and cooling balance.
  6. Condition samples consistently before deciding whether a change worked.

Process changes can reduce warpage, but they cannot always correct a fundamentally unbalanced flow pattern. Mold-flow simulation, a gate relocation, rib redesign, or a lower-warpage material system may be needed. The most useful supplier discussion includes a marked drawing and photos showing the direction and magnitude of distortion.

How to improve exposed fiber and surface quality

Glass-filled nylon will not normally reproduce the same surface as an unfilled resin. Expectations should be defined with an approved sample, texture, gloss range, or measurable color standard. “No fiber visible” is often unrealistic for a highly reinforced, dark, smooth, or complex part.

A warmer mold can help the polymer skin reproduce the tool surface before fibers freeze at the flow front. Injection-speed profiling may keep the front stable and reduce hesitation. Correct melt temperature and sufficient venting can improve fill, while excessive temperature or shear can damage the resin. Gate location influences where fibers appear and where weld lines form. A textured tool can visually manage fiber pattern better than a high-gloss surface.

Do not raise temperature without limits to chase appearance. Long residence time, dead spots, and over-heating can cause degradation, gas, deposits, odor, discoloration, and reduced properties. Use the grade supplier's processing window and examine whether the material was dried correctly before adjusting the process.

Drying, moisture, and brittle-part investigations

PA66 pellets absorb moisture after packaging is opened. During melting, excessive water can cause hydrolytic chain scission and lower molecular weight. Symptoms may include viscosity change, flash, unstable filling, surface streaks, and reduced toughness. Because reinforced compounds can still fill a cavity while degrading, appearance alone is not proof of correct drying.

Drying records should include resin lot, bag-open time, dryer type, set temperature, actual air temperature, dew point where available, drying duration, hopper loading, and conveying method. The exact target comes from the grade's processing guide. Over-drying or excessive heat exposure can also create problems, especially for pigmented or flame-retardant formulations.

A brittle part is not automatically “too dry.” Dry-as-molded PA66 is naturally stiffer and less ductile than conditioned material. Other causes include sharp corners, knit lines, excessive fiber orientation, material degradation, cold slug, contamination, molded-in stress, insufficient impact modification, or an assembly operation that exceeds the design load. Compare fracture surfaces and locations. A repeated break at the same weld line requires a different response from random breaks across several cavities.

Dimensional drift and measurement timing

A dimension is incomplete without a measurement method and time. PA66 parts can change after ejection as temperature equalizes, crystallization continues, residual stress relaxes, and moisture is absorbed. A part measured at the machine may not match one measured after 24 hours or after conditioning. International shipments add another variable because temperature and humidity can change during transit.

For critical dimensions, the control plan should define the gauge, datum scheme, fixture, sample temperature, time after molding, and conditioning state. The drawing should distinguish dimensions needed for immediate assembly from dimensions required at equilibrium. Keep approved reference parts in controlled storage and avoid comparing a new dry part with an old conditioned master.

If dimensions vary by cavity, investigate runner balance, venting, cooling, and local tool conditions. If all cavities shift together by lot or day, investigate material moisture, viscosity, dryer performance, process setup, and ambient conditioning. Part weight is a useful process indicator but cannot explain every orientation-related change.

Short shots, burn marks, and weak weld lines

Short shots can result from insufficient shot size, pressure or velocity limits, restrictive gates, low melt or mold temperature, frozen flow, poor venting, machine check-ring leakage, or a material viscosity mismatch. A fill study using staged shot sizes can show how the flow front develops and where air is trapped. Pressure curves and actual cushion are more informative than a single machine setpoint.

Burn marks near the end of fill often indicate compressed gas or inadequate venting, although degraded material can also discolor. Clean and measure vents before increasing process severity. Weak weld lines may improve with temperature, speed, venting, or gate changes, but the weld line should also be moved away from latches, screw bosses, or load paths when possible.

For electrical parts, a visually acceptable weld line may still fail terminal insertion, torque, or impact testing. Include the real assembly load in sample qualification.

Factory trial and shipment-control plan

Before the trial

Agree on the exact material grade, color, lot, packaging history, and drying procedure. Mark critical dimensions and cosmetic surfaces on the drawing. Define sample quantity, cavity identification, conditioning, and pass/fail tests. Confirm that the machine shot capacity, screw design, nozzle, hot runner, and tool materials are appropriate for reinforced nylon.

During the trial

Start from the supplier's recommended processing range. Record actual values rather than only setup targets. Establish a stable fill, then optimize packing and cooling. Collect samples at the beginning, middle, and end of the stable run. Record part weight by cavity and retain resin and molded-part samples.

After the trial

Inspect appearance and dimensions at defined times. Perform assembly, torque, impact, heat, humidity, chemical, or functional tests that reflect customer requirements. Document deviations and avoid approving a sample based only on a photograph. If a process window is narrow, conduct a small robustness study around critical settings.

Before shipment

Verify lot labels, bag sealing, pallet protection, quantity, documents, and loading condition. For moisture-sensitive material, packaging damage should trigger investigation. Agree on certificate content and retention-sample duration. Shipment inspection should confirm identity and agreed controls without implying that every property has been retested for every batch.

Questions to ask a reinforced PA66 supplier

  • What is the exact base polymer, nominal reinforcement, and modification direction?
  • Which properties are reported dry-as-molded and which are conditioned?
  • What drying and processing window applies to this exact grade?
  • What shrinkage data are available in flow and transverse directions?
  • Are heat stabilization, impact modification, flame retardancy, or color part of the grade?
  • What lot traceability, certificate, packaging, and change notification are provided?
  • Can samples be supplied from material representative of commercial production?
  • What application information is required before recommending an alternative grade?

A better supplier complaint and corrective-action workflow

When a defect appears, quarantine affected resin and parts without mixing lots. Preserve bag labels, an unopened bag where possible, molded samples from good and bad production, and the machine record. State the quantity affected and the commercial impact, but keep the first technical report factual. “Warp exceeds 1.2 mm at datum C after 24 hours” is more actionable than “material quality is unstable.”

Compare the complaint lot with the approved reference under the same drying, mold, machine, process, and conditioning plan. If production changed machine, cavity, pigment, regrind ratio, tool maintenance, or measurement timing, include that information. The purpose is not to remove supplier responsibility; it is to prevent a false conclusion and shorten the path to containment.

A corrective-action response should distinguish immediate containment, root-cause evidence, corrective action, and verification. If material is identified as the cause, define how replacement stock will be identified and how future batches will be controlled. If the process or mold is the cause, update the approved setup sheet and training. If the cause remains uncertain, agree on a controlled trial rather than releasing production on assumptions.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best mold temperature for PA66 GF30?

There is no universal setting. The correct range depends on the exact grade, surface and crystallinity targets, geometry, and tool. Start with the material manufacturer's processing guide and verify actual tool-surface temperature.

Why does glass-filled nylon warp more than expected?

Fiber orientation creates directional shrinkage. Gate position, geometry, cooling balance, packing, and conditioning determine whether those shrinkage differences balance or bend the part.

Can more glass fiber solve dimensional problems?

Not necessarily. More glass may lower shrinkage but increase anisotropy and orientation-driven warpage. A mineral-filled, hybrid-filled, lower-warpage, or redesigned system may perform better.

Why are fibers visible on black molded parts?

High contrast, smooth surfaces, low mold temperature, flow-front behavior, gate position, and formulation can reveal fibers. Set a realistic visual standard and optimize grade, texture, gate, and process together.

How should PA66 resin be stored?

Keep original packaging sealed, dry, clean, and protected from damage and extreme conditions. After opening, control exposure and dry according to the exact grade's instructions before molding.

What data should accompany a molding complaint?

Provide grade and lot, defect photos and location, cavity, dates, packaging condition, drying record, machine and mold, process settings, part weight, measurement timing, test method, and approved reference data.

Conclusion: troubleshoot the system, not one variable

Glass-filled PA66 performance comes from the interaction of resin, moisture, fiber orientation, part geometry, mold, process, conditioning, and inspection. The fastest route to a stable solution is a disciplined record of evidence followed by controlled trials. That approach also improves supplier communication because the discussion moves from “the material is bad” to a testable root-cause plan.
